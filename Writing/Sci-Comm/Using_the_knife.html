A guide to amateur cooking. Part 1: French Classical Knife Skills

Culinary · Article · 09.04.2026 (revised) · 15.12.2020 (original)
"Anyone can cook, but only the fearless can be great." — Chef Auguste Gusteau (Ratatouille)

If there is one silver lining to the period of isolation during the pandemic, it is that people finally had time for themselves. A world with social distancing and no schools, colleges, or offices got isolating very quickly. With long hours at home and restaurants being shut, it became a period of renaissance for the home kitchen. Like many others in 2020, I found myself captivated by the high-pressure culinary world of MasterChef Australia. Watching contestants execute flawless plates under the clock inspired me to move beyond basic survival cooking and towards actual technique.

It is important to note that the skills I am detailing here are rooted in the French Classical system, the global standard for professional Western kitchens. While these methods differ significantly from traditional Indian tools like the boti or aruvamana, they provide a mathematical precision that is essential for modern globalised gastronomy.

Cutting, chopping, slicing, and dicing; why are there so many terms for something as simple as using a knife? The answer is straightforward: they are not the same. Each technique serves a specific purpose. In this article, we will look at the most common knife skills used in food preparation. One of the most fundamental aspects of cooking is the ability to use a knife properly. The way ingredients are cut directly affects how they cook, how they combine with other ingredients, and ultimately, how the dish turns out.

1. The Foundation: Safety and Stability

Before the blade meets the board, three pillars of safety must be established. In this tradition, a dull knife is considered more dangerous than a sharp one, as it requires excessive force and is prone to "skating" off the surface of ingredients.

  • A Sharp Blade: A sharp edge bites into the produce, ensuring the blade travels exactly where intended.
  • A Stable Surface: A sliding cutting board is a primary cause of accidents. One must "anchor" the board by placing a damp paper towel or a thin cloth underneath to ensure a stationary surface.
  • The French Claw: To protect the guide hand, the fingers must be curled inward. The vertical section of the knuckles acts as a physical buffer and guide for the side of the blade, preventing the edge from meeting the fingertips.

2. Fundamental Cuts

A. Chopping

This is the most basic technique and does not require precision. The knife moves in a rocking motion over the ingredient until it breaks down into roughly equal pieces. This is typical of home cooking, especially in dishes where ingredients cook for long periods. The goal here is not uniformity, but simply to break the ingredient down.

B. Slicing

This is the technique used to cut across fruits, vegetables, bread, or cheese into uniform and thin pieces. This is typically used for roasts, salads, and sandwiches.

C. Mincing

This technique demands a steady hand and a sharp knife. Here, the ingredients are cut into pieces of about 1/16" (~1.6 mm), which is about as fine as one can go without producing a purée. Onions, garlic, and herbs are generally minced for seasoning or stir-frying. By mincing garlic or shallots, you maximise surface area, allowing essential oils to release and infuse into fats almost instantaneously.

3. Linear Cuts

A. Julienne

Also known as matchstick cut, this technique involves cutting vegetables into thin long strips of 1/8" (~3.2 mm) x 1/8" (~3.2 mm) x 1" to 2" (~25.4 mm to ~50.8 mm). Julienne cut is used only on firm vegetables like bell peppers, carrots, celery, and potatoes for stir-frying, as it ensures that the vegetable is cooked rapidly and evenly and mixes well with other ingredients. It also serves as the starting point for a Brunoise cut.

A variation of this technique is the Fine Julienne cut where the dimensions are 1/16" (~1.6 mm) x 1/16" (~1.6 mm) x 1" to 2" (~25.4 mm to ~50.8 mm), with the julienned vegetable being cut again along the length to form four identical pieces. This is also the starting point of a Fine Brunoise cut.

NOTE: Julienne used on a potato is known as an Allumette cut.

B. Batonnet

Commonly known as a french fry cut, this technique is used for french fries, vegetable sticks, and crudités. It involves cutting the vegetables into squares and then making them into strips of 1/4" (~6.4 mm) x 1/4" (~6.4 mm) x 2" to 2.5" (~50.8 mm to ~63.5 mm). This also serves as the starting point for a Small Dice.

4. Cube Cuts

Cubes are derived from their linear counterparts. Mastering these allows for professional presentation and even heat distribution in soups and sautés.

A. Brunoise

To execute this technique, gather julienned vegetables and cut them into small cubes of 1/8" (~3.2 mm). A variant of this is fine brunoise, where the cubes are 1/16" (~1.6 mm). This is the finest cube that we can get, and anything finer would be a mince. This cut is most often used for making sauces or aromatic garnishes and is ideal for onion, celery, bell peppers, tomato, and root vegetables.

B. Dice

A dice is a cut that creates uniform cubes and is typically used in dishes like soups and fried rice, where a number of vegetables are added. There are three variations depending on the size of the cube: Small Dice, Medium Dice, and Large Dice. Each of these cuts has its own uses in cooking.

  1. Small dice: The small dice is derived from a batonnet, and the final dimension of the cubes is 1/4" (~6.4 mm). Small diced vegetables are used in soups.
  2. Medium dice: In this technique, the final dimension of the cubes is 1/2" (~12.7 mm). Medium diced vegetables are used in chowders and fried rice.
  3. Large dice: In this technique, the final dimension of the cubes is 3/4" (~19 mm). Large diced vegetables are used in pies and stews.

5. Flat and Angled Cuts

A. Paysanne

This is a flat cut used to produce thin, even pieces of vegetables, usually about 1/2" (~12.7 mm) wide and 1/8" (~3.2 mm) thick. The shape may be square, round, or triangular depending on the original form of the vegetable. Paysanne is commonly used in soups, stews, and rustic preparations where the vegetables need to cook quickly while still retaining some visible shape.

B. Rondelle

This cut produces round slices and is most commonly used for cylindrical vegetables such as carrots, cucumbers, courgettes, and leeks. The thickness may vary depending on the dish, but the defining feature is the circular shape. Rondelle is frequently used in salads, sautés, and soups where visual uniformity is important.

6. Specialty Techniques

A. Chiffonade

Reserved for leafy greens and delicate herbs like basil. By stacking, rolling, and slicing, you create ribbons that preserve the herb's integrity rather than bruising the leaves with a heavy chop.

B. Supreming

A refined method for citrus. By removing the bitter membranes and pith, you are left with pure fruit "supremes", essential for professional salads and desserts where texture is paramount.
