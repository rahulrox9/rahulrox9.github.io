Kālavidyā: The Science of Time in Sanātana Dharma | Part 1: The Anatomy of Time

English · Sanskrit · Article · Indic · Vedic Science · 06.04.2026 (revised) · 29.03.2026 (original)

The Concept of Time

The modern understanding of time, rooted in Western thought, presents it as a straight line. It begins, moves forward, and eventually ends. In Sanātana Dharma (the Indian tradition), time is understood differently. It is not a line but a cycle. This idea is called kālacakraḥ (कालचक्रः), the wheel of time. In this view, time or kālaḥ (कालः) has no true beginning and no final end. What appears to end is simply part of a larger, repeating cycle.

अनादिर्भगवान् कालो नान्तोऽस्य द्विज विद्यते । अव्युच्छिन्नास्ततस्त्वेते सर्गस्थित्यन्तसंयमाः ॥ ~ विष्णुपुराणम् (१.२.२६) | Viṣṇupurāṇam (1.2.26) 1

This verse expresses the nature of kālaḥ with precision. Kālaḥ is described as anādiḥ (अनादिः), without beginning, and anantaḥ (अनन्तः), without end. The processes of sargaḥ (सर्गः), i.e., creation; sthitiḥ (स्थितिः), i.e., maintenance; and antaḥ (अन्तः), i.e., dissolution, are described as avyucchinnāḥ (अव्युछिन्नाः), meaning uninterrupted.

In other words, creation, sustenance, and dissolution are not separate events, but continuous phases within kālacakraḥ.

The Classification of Time

लोकानामन्तकृत् कालः कालोऽन्यः कलनात्मकः । स द्विधा स्थूलसूक्ष्मत्वान् मूर्तश्चामूर्त उच्यते ॥ १. १० ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१०) | Sūryasiddhāntaḥ (1.10) 2a

The Sūryasiddhāntaḥ describes kālaḥ as being of two types. The first is lokāntakṛt (लोकान्तकृत्), that which brings about the end of worlds, referring to the force leading to pralayaḥ (प्रलयः). The second is kalanātmakaḥ (कलनात्मकः), measurable time, derived from the root कल् (kal), meaning “to count.” This is time as something that can be structured and calculated.

Within kalanātmakaḥ kālaḥ, a further distinction is made based on perceptibility. Mūrtaḥ (मूर्तः) refers to time that is perceptible, grounded in motion, breath, and observable processes. Amūrtaḥ (अमूर्तः) refers to extremely subtle units, such as truṭiḥ (त्रुटिः), which lie beyond direct perception but are conceptually defined.

The Dimensions of Mūrta Time

प्राणादिः कथितो मूर्तस्त्रुट्याद्योऽमूर्तसंज्ञकः । षड्भिः प्राणैर्विनाडी स्यात् तत्षष्ट्या नाडिका स्मृता ॥ १.११ ॥
नाडीषष्ट्या तु नाक्षत्रमहोरात्रं प्रकीर्तितम् । तत्त्रिंशता भवेन् मासः सावनोऽर्कोदयैस्तथा ॥ १.१२ ॥
ऐन्दवस्तिथिभिस्तद्वत् सङ्क्रान्त्या सौर उच्यते । मासैर्द्वादशभिर्वर्षं दिव्यं तदह उच्यते ॥ १.१३ ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.११-१.१३) | Sūryasiddhāntaḥ (1.11-1.13) 2b

Time, in this system, is built progressively from smaller perceptible units to larger cycles.

Prāṇa (प्राणः) and related units are termed mūrta (मूर्तः), while truṭi (त्रुटिः) and smaller units are termed amūrta (अमूर्तः). From this starting point, larger units are constructed step by step. Six prāṇa make one vināḍī (विनाडी), and sixty vināḍī make one nāḍikā (नाडिका). Sixty nāḍikā constitute one nākṣatram ahorātram (नाक्षत्रम् अहोरात्रम्), a sidereal day-night cycle.

From a day, the system extends further into months. Thirty such ahorātram form one sāvanaḥ māsaḥ (सावनः मासः), a month consisting of thirty sunrise-to-sunrise days. Two other types of māsa are also described. The aindavaḥ māsaḥ (ऐन्दवः मासः) is determined by tithi (तिथि), a lunar day defined by the angular separation between the sun and the moon. The sauraḥ māsaḥ (सौरः मासः) is determined by saṅkrāntiḥ (सङ्क्रान्तिः), the transition of the sun into a new rāśi.

These systems culminate in the measurement of the year. Twelve sāvanaḥ māsaḥ constitute one varṣam (वर्षम्), which is regarded as a divyam ahaḥ (दिव्यम् अहः), a day of the deva.

The Scaling of Cosmic Cycles

सुरासुराणामन्योन्यमहोरात्रं विपर्ययात् । तत्षष्टिः षड्गुणा दिव्यं वर्षमासुरमेव च ॥ १.१४ ॥
तद्द्वादशसहस्राणि चतुर्युगमुदाहृतम् । सूर्याब्दसङ्ख्यया द्वित्रिसागरैरयुताहतैः ॥ १.१५ ॥
सन्ध्यासन्ध्यांशसहितं विज्ञेयं तच्चतुर्युगम् । कृतादीनां व्यवस्थेयं धर्मपादव्यवस्थया ॥ १.१६ ॥
युगस्य दशमो भागश्चतुस्त्रिद्व्येकसङ्गुणः । क्रमात् कृतयुगादीनां षष्ठांशः सन्ध्ययोः स्वकः ॥ १.१७ ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१४-१.१७) | Sūryasiddhāntaḥ (1.14-1.17) 2c

This section describes how time, once measured, is further scaled into vast cosmic cycles.

The day of the Surāḥ (Devāḥ) corresponds to the night of the Asurāḥ, and vice versa. Three hundred and sixty such human (or solar) years constitute one “divine” year. Twelve thousand such “divine” years make one caturyugam (चतुर्युगम्). In counts of solar (or human) years, this amounts to 4,320,000 years.

This caturyugam, including its transitional phases – sandhyā (सन्ध्या) and sandhyāṃśa (सन्ध्यांश) – is structured according to the progressive decline of dharmaḥ (धर्मः). The four yuga – kṛta (कृत), tretā (त्रेता), dvāpara (द्वापर), and kali (कलि) – are proportioned in the ratio 4:3:2:1. Each is derived as a multiple of 432,000 years, with one-sixth of its duration constituting its transitional phases.

तच्छतं तस्य वर्षाणां परमायुर्निरूपितम् । तदर्धं व्यतीतं तस्य विंशत्संख्ये सति स्थिते ॥ १.२१ ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१८-१.२१) | Sūrya Siddhānta (1.18-1.20) 2d

The system then extends further into larger cosmic cycles.

Seventy-one caturyugam constitute one manvantara (मन्वन्तरम्), the period of a Manu. At the end of each manvantara occurs a transitional phase equal in duration to a Kṛta Yuga (1,728,000 years), associated with jalaplavaḥ (जलप्लवः), the great flood.

Fourteen such manvantara, along with their transitional phases, constitute one kalpa (कल्प), a day of Brahmā or brāhmam-ahaḥ (ब्राह्ममहः). The śarvarī (शर्वरी), i.e., night, of Brahmā is of equal duration. An additional initial transition of the same duration occurs at the beginning of the kalpa. In total, a kalpa comprises one thousand caturyugam and represents a complete cycle of creation and dissolution.

Current temporal coordinates

परमायुः शतं तस्य तयाहोरात्रसङ्ख्यया । आयुषोऽर्धमितं तस्य शेषकल्पोऽयमादिमः ॥ १. २१ ॥
कल्पादस्माच्च मनवः षड् व्यतीताः ससन्धयः । वैवस्वतस्य च मनोर्युगानां त्रिघनो गतः ॥ १.२२ ॥
अष्टाविंशाद्युगादस्माद्यातमेतत् कृतं युगम् । अतः कालं प्रसङ्ख्याय सङ्ख्यामेकत्र पिण्डयेत् ॥ १.२३ ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.२१-१.२३) | Sūrya Siddhānta (1.21-1.23) 2e

Of Brahmā’s centennial lifespan, exactly half (āyuṣo’rdhamitaṃ, आयुषोऽर्धमितं) has elapsed. The present kalpaḥ is the first (ādimah, आदिमः) of the remaining period, known as the Śvetavarāha-kalpaḥ (श्वेतवाराहकल्पः).

Within this kalpa, six Manavaḥ (मनवः), along with their respective sandhyāḥ, have already elapsed. We are currently in the reign of the seventh Manu, Vaivasvata (वैवस्वतः), within which twenty-seven caturyugam have passed.

In the present, twenty-eighth caturyugam (अष्टाविंशतितमं युगम्), we are in the Kali Yuga (कलियुगम्), specifically in its first quarter (प्रथमपादः).
