Kālavidyā: The Science of Time in Sanātana Dharma | Part 1: The Anatomy of Time

English · Sanskrit · Article · Indic · Vedic Science · 29.03.2026

The Concept of Time

The modern understanding of time, rooted in Western thought, follows a linear model. Time begins at a fixed point, moves in a single direction, and tends toward an inevitable end. In contrast, Sanātana Dharma presents the idea of कालचक्रः (kālacakraḥ), the wheel of time. Here, time is understood as कालः (kālaḥ), which has neither an absolute beginning nor a final end. What appears as an ending is simply a transition within an ongoing cycle. सर्गः (sargaḥ), स्थितिः (sthitiḥ), and अन्तः (antaḥ) unfold repeatedly in an unbroken continuum.

अनादिर्भगवान् कालो नान्तोऽस्य द्विज विद्यते । अव्युच्छिन्नास्ततस्त्वेते सर्गस्थित्यन्तसंयमाः ॥ ~ विष्णुपुराणम् (१.२.२६) | Viṣṇupurāṇam (1.2.26) 1

This verse expresses the nature of kālaḥ succinctly. Kālaḥ is described as अनादिः, without beginning, and अनन्तः, without end. The processes of sargaḥ, sthitiḥ, and antaḥ are अव्युच्छिन्नाः, uninterrupted. These are not isolated events, but continuous phases within kālacakraḥ.

The Classification of Time

लोकानामन्तकृत् कालः कालोऽन्यः कलनात्मकः । स द्विधा स्थूलसूक्ष्मत्वान् मूर्तश्चामूर्त उच्यते ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१०) | Sūryasiddhāntaḥ (1.10) 2a

The Sūryasiddhāntaḥ distinguishes kālaḥ in two ways. The first is लोकान्तकृत् (lokāntakṛt), that which brings about the end of worlds, referring to the force leading to प्रलयः (pralayaḥ). The second is कलनात्मकः (kalanātmakaḥ), measurable time, derived from the root कल्, to count. This is time as something that can be structured and calculated.

Within kalanātmakaḥ kālaḥ, a further distinction is made based on perceptibility. मूर्तः (mūrtaḥ) refers to time that is perceptible, grounded in motion, breath, and observable processes. अमूर्तः (amūrtaḥ) refers to extremely subtle units, such as त्रुटिः (truṭiḥ), which lie beyond direct perception but are conceptually defined.

The Dimensions of Mūrta Time

प्राणादिः कथितो मूर्तस्त्रुट्याद्योऽमूर्तसंज्ञकः । षड्भिः प्राणैर्विनाडी स्यात् तत्षष्ट्या नाडिका स्मृता ॥ Prāṇa and related units are termed mūrta; truṭi and smaller units are termed amūrta. Six prāṇa make one vināḍī, and sixty vināḍī make one nāḍikā. नाडीषष्ट्या तु नाक्षत्रमहोरात्रं प्रकीर्तितम् । तत्त्रिंशता भवेन् मासः सावनोऽर्कोदयैस्तथा ॥ Sixty nāḍikā constitute one नाक्षत्रम् अहोरात्रम् (nākṣatram ahorātram), a sidereal day-night cycle. Thirty such ahorātram form one सावनः मासः (sāvanaḥ māsaḥ), a month made up of 30 sunrise-to-sunrise days. ऐन्दवस्तिथिभिस्तद्वत् सङ्क्रान्त्या सौर उच्यते । मासैर्द्वादशभिर्वर्षं दिव्यं तदह उच्यते ॥ Two other types of māsa are described: the ऐन्दवः (aindavaḥ) māsaḥ is determined by तिथि (tithi), a lunar day defined by the angular separation between the sun and the moon; the सौरः (sauraḥ) māsaḥ is determined by सङ्क्रान्तिः (saṅkrāntiḥ), the transition of the sun into a new rāśi. Twelve [sāvanaḥ] māsaḥ constitute a वर्षम् (varṣam), which is regarded as a दिव्यम् अहः (divyam ahaḥ), a day of the deva. ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.११-१.१३) | Sūrya Siddhānta (1.11-1.13) 2b

The Scaling of Cosmic Cycles

सुरासुराणामन्योन्यमहोरात्रं विपर्ययात् । तत्षष्टिः षड्गुणा दिव्यं वर्षमासुरमेव च ॥ The day and night of the Surāḥ (Devāḥ) and the Asurāḥ are mutually opposed. Six times sixty [तत्षष्टिः षड्गुणा; 360] of these [human years] make a "divine" year. तद्द्वादशसहस्राणि चतुर्युगमुदाहृतम् । सूर्याब्दसङ्ख्यया द्वित्रिसागरैरयुताहतैः ॥ Twelve thousand such "divine" years constitute one चतुर्युगम् (caturyugam). In सूर्याब्दसङ्ख्या (sūryābdasaṅkhyā) or in solar years, this amounts to 432 (द्वित्रिसागरैः, dvitrisāgaraiḥ) multiplied by 10,000 (अयुत, ayuta), yielding 4,320,000. सन्ध्यासन्ध्यांशसहितं विज्ञेयं तच्चतुर्युगम् । कृतादीनां व्यवस्थेयं धर्मपादव्यवस्थया ॥
युगस्य दशमो भागश्चतुस्त्रिद्व्येकसङ्गुणः । क्रमात् कृतयुगादीनां षष्ठांशः सन्ध्ययोः स्वकः ॥ This caturyugam, including its transitional phases (सन्ध्या, sandhyā; सन्ध्यांश, sandhyāṃśa), is structured according to the progressive decline of धर्मः (dharmaḥ). The duration of the four yugam: कृत (kṛta), त्रेता (tretā), द्वापर (dvāpara), and कलि (kali), is determined as four, three, two, and one multiples respectively of one-tenth of the Caturyuga (432,000). One-sixth of each yugam constitutes its transitional phases.

युगानां सप्ततिः सैका मन्वन्तरमिहोच्यते । कृताब्दसङ्ख्या तस्यान्ते सन्धिः प्रोक्तो जलप्लवः ॥ Seventy-one caturyugam constitute a मन्वन्तरम् (manvantara), the period of a Manu.
