Kāla-Vidhyā: The Science of Time in Sanātana Dharma | Part 1: The Anatomy of Time

English · Sanskrit · Article · Indic · Vedic Science · 29.03.2026

The Concept of Time

The modern understanding of time, rooted in Western thought, follows a linear model: time begins at a fixed point (say, the Big Bang), moves in a single direction, and tends toward an inevitable end. Sanātana Dharma offers something altogether different, the कालचक्र or the wheel of time. In this framework, time has no definitive start, no terminal conclusion; every ending is simply the precursor to a new beginning. What the linear view calls a terminus, the cyclical view recognises as a threshold.

अनादिर्भगवान् कालो नान्तोऽस्य द्विज विद्यते । अव्युच्छिन्नास्ततस्त्वेते सर्गस्थित्यन्तसंयमाः ॥ ~ विष्णुपुराणम् (१.२.२६) | Viṣṇupurāṇam (1.2.26) 1

This verse from the Viṣṇupurāṇam encapsulates the idea precisely: time (कालः) has neither a beginning (अनादिः) nor an end (नान्तः), and the unbroken (अव्युच्छिन्नाः) succession of creation (सर्ग), maintenance (स्थिति), and dissolution (अन्त) turns without interruption; each cycle giving way seamlessly to the next.

The Classification of Time

लोकानामन्तकृत् कालः कालोऽन्यः कलनात्मकः । स द्विधा स्थूलसूक्ष्मत्वान् मूर्तश्चामूर्त उच्यते ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१०) | Sūryasiddhānta (1.10) 2a

This verse from the Sūryasiddhānta classifies time along two axes: its role or function, and its scale or dimension. By function, time is of two kinds. The first is Lokāntakṛt (लोकानामन्तकृत्), literally "the finisher of worlds", denoting Cosmic Time: the irreversible, entropic force that drives the universe toward its eventual dissolution, or Pralaya. The second is Kalanātmaka (कलनात्मकः), meaning "calculable time" and derived from the root kal (to count). This is time as a measurable quantity, the domain of mathematics and astronomy.

Within Kalanātmaka time, scale introduces a further distinction. Mūrta (मूर्त) refers to time units that are perceptible, grounded in physical movement or biological cycles and observable to human senses. Amūrta (अमूर्त) denotes infinitesimal or atomic units that fall below the threshold of human perception, representing time at its most fundamental, indivisible level.

The Dimensions of Mūrta Time

प्राणादिः कथितो मूर्तस्त्रुट्याद्योऽमूर्तसंज्ञकः । षड्भिः प्राणैर्विनाडी स्यात् तत्षष्ट्या नाडिका स्मृता ॥ Prāṇa and the rest are termed mūrta; Truṭi and the rest are termed amūrta. Six Prāṇas (human breath) make a Vināḍī; sixty of those are considered a Nāḍikā. नाडीषष्ट्या तु नाक्षत्रमहोरात्रं प्रकीर्तितम् । तत्त्रिंशता भवेन् मासः सावनोऽर्कोदयैस्तथा ॥ Sixty Nāḍikās are declared to be a sidereal day-night (nākṣatram-ahorātram). Thirty of such [days] make a Sāvana [civil] month, measured by sunrises (arkodayaiḥ). ऐन्दवस्तिथिभिस्तद्वत् सङ्क्रान्त्या सौर उच्यते । मासैर्द्वादशभिर्वर्षं दिव्यं तदह उच्यते ॥ A lunar [aindava] month is regulated by lunar days [tithi]; a solar [saura] month is determined by the sun's entrance into a zodiac sign [saṅkrānti]. Twelve months make a year; that is called a day of the gods [divyam-ahaḥ]. ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.११-१.१३) | Sūryasiddhānta (1.11-1.13) 2b

The Scaling of Cosmic Cycles

सुरासुराणामन्योन्यमहोरात्रं विपर्ययात् । तत्षष्टिः षड्गुणा दिव्यं वर्षमासुरमेव च ॥ The day and night of the Surāḥ (Devāḥ) and the Asurāḥ are mutually exclsuive. Six times sixty [360] of these [human years] make a "divine" year. तद्द्वादशसहस्राणि चतुर्युगमुदाहृतम् । सूर्याब्दसङ्ख्यया द्वित्रिसागरैरयुताहतैः ॥ Twelve thousand of these [divine years] are called a Caturyugaḥ (चतुर्युगः). In the count of solar years, this is 432 (द्वित्रिसागरैगः/dvitrisāgaraiaḥ) multiplied (आहतः/āhataḥ) by 10,000 (ayuta), yielding 4,320,000. सन्ध्यासन्ध्यांशसहितं विज्ञेयं तच्चतुर्युगम् । कृतादीनां व्यवस्थेयं धर्मपादव्यवस्थया ॥
युगस्य दशमो भागश्चतुस्त्रिद्व्येकसङ्गुणः । क्रमात् कृतयुगादीनां षष्ठांशः सन्ध्ययोः स्वकः ॥ This Caturyuga, including the transitions at the beginning (सन्ध्या/sandhyā) and end (सन्ध्यांशगः/sandhyāṃśa) including its beginning of an age (सन्ध्या/sandhyā) and end of an age (सन्ध्यांशगः/sandhyāṃśa), is understood to be divided into Kṛta (कृत) and the other [three] ages (viz., त्रेता/tretā, द्वापर/dvāpara, and कलि/kali) based on the systematic proportion of the quarters of Dharma (धर्मपादव्यवस्थया/dharmapādavyavasthayā). The 4th, 3rd, 2nd and 1st multiple (चतुस्त्रिद्व्येकसङ्गुणः/catustridvyekasaṅguṇaḥ), respectively, of the 10th part (दशमो भागः/daśamo bhāgaḥ) of the Caturyugaḥ (432,000) determines the duration of the individual yuga. One-sixth (षष्ठांशः/ṣaṣṭhāṃśaḥ) of each yuga represents its respective transition periods.
