Kāla-Vidhyā: The Science of Time in Sanātana Dharma
Part 1: The Anatomy of Time

Indic · Article · VedicScience · 28.03.2026 (updated)

The concept of Time

The modern understanding of time, rooted in Western thought, follows a linear model: time begins at a fixed point (say, the Big Bang), moves in a single direction, and tends toward an inevitable end. Sanātana Dharma offers something altogether different, the कालचक्र or the wheel of time. In this framework, time has no definitive start, no terminal conclusion; every ending is simply the precursor to a new beginning. What the linear view calls a terminus, the cyclical view recognises as a threshold.

अनादिर्भगवान् कालो नान्तोऽस्य द्विज विद्यते । अव्युच्छिन्नास्ततस्त्वेते सर्गस्थित्यन्तसंयमाः ॥ ~ विष्णुपुराणम् (१.२.२६) | Viṣṇupurāṇam (1.2.26) 1

This verse from the Viṣṇupurāṇam encapsulates the idea precisely: time (कालः) has neither a beginning (अनादिः) nor an end (नान्तः), and the unbroken (अव्युच्छिन्नाः) succession of creation (सर्ग), maintenance (स्थिति), and dissolution (अन्त) turns without interruption; each cycle giving way seamlessly to the next.

The Classification of Time

लोकानामन्तकृत् कालः कालोऽन्यः कलनात्मकः ।
स द्विधा स्थूलसूक्ष्मत्वान् मूर्तश्चामूर्त उच्यते ॥ ~ सूर्यसिद्धान्तः (१.१०) | Sūryasiddhānta (1.10) 2a

This verse from the Sūryasiddhānta classifies time along two axes: its role or function, and its scale or dimension. By function, time is of two kinds. The first is Lokāntakṛt (लोकानामन्तकृत्), literally "the finisher of worlds", denoting Cosmic Time: the irreversible, entropic force that drives the universe toward its eventual dissolution, or Pralaya. The second is Kalanātmaka (कलनात्मकः), meaning "calculable time" and derived from the root kal (to count). This is time as a measurable quantity, the domain of mathematics and astronomy.

Within Kalanātmaka time, scale introduces a further distinction. Mūrta (मूर्त) refers to time units that are perceptible, grounded in physical movement or biological cycles and observable to human senses. Amūrta (अमूर्त) denotes infinitesimal or atomic units that fall below the threshold of human perception, representing time at its most fundamental, indivisible level.
