The Living Memory: Tulasīdāsa and the Hanuman Chalisa To understand the true origin of the Hanuman Chalisa, one must first turn to the living oral tradition that has resonated across the Indian subcontinent for centuries. As I received this account, passed down through generations of devotees following the ancient paramparā, the hymn was not merely composed as a literary exercise. It was born out of a moment of intense spiritual trial, divine intervention, and unwavering faith. According to this revered tradition, the Mughal Emperor Akbar heard of Goswami Tulsīdās's immense spiritual reputation and summoned him to the royal court at Fatehpur Sikri. The Emperor, surrounded by the immense wealth and military might of his empire, demanded that the saint perform a miracle to prove his spiritual powers. Tulasīdāsa, embodying the true humility of a Bhakta, refused. He famously stated that he possessed no magical powers and was merely a humble servant of Lord Rama. Angered by what he perceived as defiance, Akbar ordered the saint to be imprisoned. It was within the dark confines of his prison cell that Tulasīdāsa turned to Lord Hanuman, the ultimate remover of obstacles. Over the course of his captivity, he composed forty intensely powerful verses of praise – the Hanuman Chalisa. Tradition holds that upon completing the final verse, a massive army of monkeys suddenly descended upon Fatehpur Sikri. They wreaked absolute havoc across the town and the imperial palace, terrorising the guards and bringing the royal court to a standstill. Realising that he had crossed a divine line and facing complete chaos, a humbled Emperor Akbar released Tulasīdāsa, begged for his forgiveness, and bowed to the power of his devotion. The Written Record: The Meeting with Akbar Below is the account from the Bhaktirasbodhinī (1712), the primary commentary on Nābhādāsa’s Bhaktamāla. This text identifies the specific moment Tulasīdāsa was summoned to the Mughal court and his subsequent refusal to perform a miracle. Braj Bhasha/Hindi Text: दिल्ली पति पातिसाह खबरि सुनाइ आई, विप्र एक कासी बीच कीनौ है सजीव जू। संवत सोरह सौ तीस माँहि नीके करि, दरसन पायो सुत दियो है सजीव जू॥ साहिब पठायो लैन आए सब खाँन पान, बोले कर जोरि तुम चलौ महाराज जू। तुलसी कहैं हम राम के गुलाम महा, काहू को न काम इहाँ राम ही सो काज जू॥ English Translation: The Emperor of Delhi heard the news that a Brāhmaṇa in Kāśī had brought someone back to life. In the year Samvat 1630, after having a divine vision, he [Tulasīdāsa] had restored a son to life. The Emperor sent his officials with all provisions, who spoke with folded hands, "O Great King, please come with us." Tulasī replied, "I am but a great slave of Rāma; I have no business with anyone else, for my only concern is with Rāma."
