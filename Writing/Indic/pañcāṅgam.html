Kāla-Vidhyā: The Science of Time in Sanātana Dharma
Part 1: पञ्चाङ्गम् (pañcāṅgam)

Indic · Article · VedicScience · 23.08.2020 (original), 28.03.2026 (updated)
तिथिवारं च नक्षत्रं योगः करणमेव च । पञ्चाङ्गमिति विख्यातं कालज्ञानस्य साधनम् ॥ - पञ्चाङ्गस्य परिभाषा (लोकप्रसिद्धः श्लोकः) Definition of the Pañcāṅga (a popularly known verse)

If there is one silver lining to the period of isolation during the pandemic, it is the renaissance of the home kitchen. Like many others in 2020, I found myself captivated by the high-pressure Indic world of MasterChef Australia. Watching contestants execute flawless plates under the clock inspired me to move beyond basic survival cooking and towards actual technique.

It is important to note that the skills I am detailing here are rooted in the French Classical system, the global standard for professional Western kitchens. While these methods differ significantly from traditional Indian tools like the boti or aruvamana, they provide a mathematical precision that is essential for modern globalised gastronomy.

1. The Foundation: Safety and Stability

Before the blade meets the board, three pillars of safety must be established. In this tradition, a dull knife is considered more dangerous than a sharp one, as it requires excessive force and is prone to "skating" off the surface of ingredients.

  • A Sharp Blade: A sharp edge bites into the produce, ensuring the blade travels exactly where intended.
  • A Stable Surface: A sliding cutting board is a primary cause of accidents. One must "anchor" the board by placing a damp paper towel or a thin cloth underneath to ensure a stationary surface.
  • The French Claw: To protect the guide hand, the fingers must be curled inward. The vertical section of the knuckles acts as a physical buffer and guide for the side of the blade, preventing the edge from meeting the fingertips.

2. The Mechanics of the Cut

Chopping & Mincing

Chopping is the workhorse of the home kitchen—a rhythmic, rocking motion where utility takes precedence over mathematical precision. It is ideal for aromatics that will be cooked down for hours in a stew or ragu.

Mincing is the pursuit of the smallest possible particle. By mincing garlic or shallots, you maximise surface area, allowing essential oils to release and infuse into fats almost instantaneously.

Linear Cuts: Julienne & Batonnet

These cuts transform irregular vegetables into uniform sticks. This uniformity ensures that every piece in a stir-fry or salad reaches the same level of crispness at the same time.

  • Julienne: 1/8" x 1/8" x 2". Often referred to as "matchsticks."
  • Batonnet: 1/4" x 1/4" x 2.5". The standard foundation for french fries.

Cube Cuts: Brunoise & Dice

Cubes are derived from their linear counterparts. Mastering these allows for professional presentation and even heat distribution in soups and sautés.

  • Brunoise: 1/8" cubes (derived from Julienne). Reserved for refined sauces and garnishes.
  • Small Dice: 1/4" cubes (derived from Batonnet). The standard for a classic mirepoix.
  • Large Dice: 3/4" cubes. Used for hearty slow-cooked stews where ingredients must withstand hours of heat without disintegrating.

3. Specialty Techniques

Chiffonade

Reserved for leafy greens and delicate herbs like basil. By stacking, rolling, and slicing, you create ribbons that preserve the herb's integrity rather than bruising the leaves with a heavy chop.

Supreming

A refined method for citrus. By removing the bitter membranes and pith, you are left with pure fruit "supremes," essential for professional salads and desserts where texture is paramount.
