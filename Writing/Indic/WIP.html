Unlocking the Forty Verses of the Hanumān Chālīsā | Part 1: Context, History, and Composition

English · Awadhi · Braj Bhasha · Article · Indic · 02.04.2026

The Enigma of Origin: Between History and Faith

Within the framework of Sanātana Dharma or Hinduism, questioning is not always seen as a departure from faith. When undertaken with sincerity, it can be a way of approaching it more closely. Traditions are not only received; they are reflected upon, revisited, and understood anew across generations. It is in that spirit that this enquiry began on the Caitra Pūrṇimā (the full moon day in March–April), which is traditionally celebrated as Hanumān Jayanti or the birth anniversary of Hanumān in the northern parts of India. Āstikas in the southern Indian state of Tamil Nadu, where I hail from, celebrate it in the month of Margazhi (Mārgaśīrṣa), which broadly corresponds to November–December, when Amāvāsyā Tithi (New Moon) coincides with the Mūla Nakṣatram. While different calendar systems lead to variation regarding Jayanthi, the Hanumān Chālīsā is unanimously revered across India for providing strength, protection, and mental peace.

Pinpointing the definitive historical origin of the Hanumān Chālīsā presents a significant challenge to the modern researcher. While its attribution to Gosvāmī Tulasīdās is undisputed within the devotional heart of India, the precise moment of its creation remains shrouded in an overlap of disparate records and centuries of reverence. To seek its beginning, one must navigate through the dry confines of academic archives and also turn toward the living memory of the Indian subcontinent, the oral tradition. Both spheres offer distinct insights that, while frequently at odds, collectively form the foundation of the hymn’s legacy.

The Oral Tradition

As I received this account, passed down through generations of devotees following the ancient paramparā, it becomes clear that this composition was not merely a literary exercise; rather, it was born out of a moment of intense spiritual trial and divine intervention. According to this widely held tradition, the Mughal Emperor Akbar, intrigued by Tulasīdās’s spiritual reputation, summoned him to the royal court at Delhi (but likely Fatehpur Sikri's Diwan-i-Am). Tulasīdās, embodying the true humility of a Bhakta, refused. He famously stated that he possessed no magical powers and was merely a humble servant of Prabhu Śrī Rāma. Angered by what he perceived as defiance, Akbar ordered the saint to be imprisoned. It was within the dark confines of his prison cell that Tulasīdās turned to Hanumān Jī to rescue him from the clutches of the Mughals. Over the course of his captivity, he composed forty intensely powerful verses of praise – the Hanumān Chālīsā. Tradition holds that upon completing the final verse, a massive army of monkeys suddenly descended upon Fatehpur Sikri. They wreaked absolute havoc across the town and the imperial palace, terrorising the guards and bringing the royal court to a standstill. Realising that he had crossed a divine line and facing complete chaos, a humbled Emperor Akbar released Tulasīdās, begged for his forgiveness, and bowed to the power of his devotion.

The Written Record: The Meeting with Akbar Below is the account from the Bhaktirasbodhinī (1712), the primary commentary on Nābhādāsa’s Bhaktamāla. This text identifies the specific moment Tulasīdās was summoned to the Mughal court and his subsequent refusal to perform a miracle. Braj Bhasha/Hindi Text: दिल्ली पति पातिसाह खबरि सुनाइ आई, विप्र एक कासी बीच कीनौ है सजीव जू। संवत सोरह सौ तीस माँहि नीके करि, दरसन पायो सुत दियो है सजीव जू॥ साहिब पठायो लैन आए सब खाँन पान, बोले कर जोरि तुम चलौ महाराज जू। तुलसी कहैं हम राम के गुलाम महा, काहू को न काम इहाँ राम ही सो काज जू॥ English Translation: The Emperor of Delhi heard the news that a Brāhmaṇa in Kāśī had brought someone back to life. In the year Samvat 1630, after having a divine vision, he [Tulasīdās] had restored a son to life. The Emperor sent his officials with all provisions, who spoke with folded hands, "O Great King, please come with us." Tulasī replied, "I am but a great slave of Rāma; I have no business with anyone else, for my only concern is with Rāma."

The Silence of the Imperial Records

Sceptics and modern empirical historians often point to a discrepancy here. They note that the official, highly detailed contemporary records of the Mughal court, such as Abu'l-Fazl’s Akbarnama and the Ain-i-Akbari make absolutely no mention of Tulasīdās being imprisoned, nor do they record a sudden plague of monkeys bringing the capital to its knees. However, to dismiss the Bhakti tradition simply because it does not appear in state-sponsored diaries fails to account for the nature of those imperial texts. There are three critical reasons why a Mughal chronicler would have intentionally omitted this event: 1. Theological Incompatibility: Court chroniclers, bound by strict monotheistic Islamic theology, were ideologically opposed to acknowledging or documenting miracles attributed to Hindu deities or saints. To record such an event would be to validate a faith outside their own orthodox framework. 2. Imperial Censorship: In the zealously orthodox environment of the Mughal court, scribes were employed to glorify the Emperor as an infallible, semi-divine ruler. They had a massive incentive to suppress or completely censor any embarrassing incident where the Emperor was humiliated, made to look weak, and forced to capitulate to the spiritual power of a local Hindu saint. Ultimately, while the official court records chose silence to protect the image of an emperor, the oral tradition chose to remember the triumph of a saint. The Hanuman Chalisa survives today not as a relic of a royal court, but as a living testament to a faith that outlasted an empire. What follows is not a scholarly commentary. It is a reader's reflection — shaped by years of listening to traditional discourses, reading the Valmiki Ramayana, and sitting with these forty verses. I make no claim to authority. I only claim attention.
